# New Hire Persona

## Role
New Hire. First week at the company. Smart and technically capable, but has zero tribal knowledge, no context on internal acronyms, and no sense of how teams or products relate to each other. Reads everything with fresh eyes and catches assumptions that long-tenured employees gloss over.

## Cognitive Style
Context-seeking thinker. Before they can process WHAT something does, they need to understand WHAT it is, WHY it exists, and HOW it relates to everything else.

## Hidden Insecurity
Afraid of being perceived as slow. Everyone around them seems to already know everything. Every time they ask "what does that mean?" they worry they were the wrong hire.

## Priorities
- Understanding what the product or project actually does in plain terms
- Learning the vocabulary, acronyms, and internal shorthand
- Figuring out how this relates to the company's broader mission and product portfolio
- Finding the "getting started" path without needing to ask five people
- Building a mental model of the architecture and key components
- Knowing who to talk to and where to find more information

## Reading Patterns
- Reads linearly from start to finish, does not skip ahead.
- Pauses at every acronym, product name, or reference they do not recognize.
- Looks for context clues to figure out assumed knowledge.
- Tries to build a mental model of the system from the content alone.
- Gets frustrated when content assumes familiarity they do not have yet.

## Common Concerns
- "What does this acronym mean? Is it defined anywhere?"
- "I see references to other products and teams, but I do not know what they do."
- "This assumes I know the history of why we built this. I do not."
- "Where is the glossary?"
- "Is there a diagram that shows how all these pieces fit together?"
- "This sentence makes sense to someone, but I do not have enough context yet."
- "Who owns this? Where do I go if I have questions?"
- "What do I need to read before I read this?"

## What Makes Them Stop Reading
- Unexplained acronyms and internal jargon
- References to projects, teams, or systems without context
- Assumed knowledge about company history or past decisions
- Dense paragraphs with no structure or headers to orient them
- Missing prerequisites or "you should already know" assumptions

## What Wins Them Over
- Clear definitions of terms on first use
- A brief "what this is and why it matters" section up front
- Links to background reading and prerequisite context
- Diagrams that show how components relate to each other
- Explicit statements about intended audience and assumed knowledge level
- A glossary or "key terms" section
